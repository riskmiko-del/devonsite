Lydford Gorge Walk
Devon's Deepest Gorge

The River Lyd has carved through the granite for millennia, creating Devon's deepest river gorge — a dramatic cleft through ancient oak woodland, with a 30-metre waterfall at one end and a roaring whirlpool at the other. One of the most spectacular short walks in the county.

Devon's Most Dramatic Woodland Walk

Lydford Gorge is managed by the National Trust and is one of the most spectacular natural landscapes in Devon — a place where the River Lyd, fed by the rainfall of the western Dartmoor moorland above, has cut down through resistant granite over thousands of years, creating a gorge that in places is barely 2 metres wide and 30 metres deep.

The walk follows the gorge from the main entrance at Lydford to the White Lady Waterfall — a straight, clean drop of about 30 metres into a rocky plunge pool — and returns along the opposite bank via the Devil's Cauldron, where the river is forced through a narrow rocky slot and compressed into a thundering, mist-shrouded whirlpool. Between these two showpieces, the path winds through one of Devon's finest ancient oak woods — mossy boulders, gnarled roots, ferns and the constant sound of water below.

🎟️ National Trust Entry

Lydford Gorge charges entry — National Trust members enter free, non-members pay a fee (check NT website for current prices). The car park is included with entry. The gorge is open daily in season (March–November) and on a limited basis in winter when the paths can become dangerous due to ice and flood. Always check opening before travelling in winter.

The Route

🗺️ Lydford Gorge Full Circular (3.5 miles · 2–3 hours)

1
Main entrance → gorge path south (1 mile)

From the main NT car park and visitor centre at Lydford, follow the gorge path south. Immediately the scale of the gorge becomes apparent — the path runs along the rim in places, with the river audible but not visible deep below through the canopy of sessile oak. The woodland here is ancient — gnarled and mossy, with ferns covering every surface, exactly the kind of atmospheric woodland that earns the term "primeval".

2
White Lady Waterfall (1 mile south of entrance)

The White Lady Waterfall is the centrepiece of the south end of the gorge — the Lyd drops in a single clean fall of about 30 metres over a granite lip into a boulder-strewn plunge pool. In dry conditions the fall is elegant; after rain it becomes a roaring torrent. A viewing platform at the base offers the best perspective. The fall is named for the white, aerated appearance of the water in spate conditions.

3
Return along west bank → Devil's Cauldron (1.5 miles north)

Cross the river at the bridge below the waterfall and return north along the opposite (western) bank. This side of the gorge is often quieter than the outbound route. After about 1.5 miles the path descends to the Devil's Cauldron — the most dramatic point on the whole walk, where the river is constricted into a narrow slot between granite walls and churned into a permanent whirlpool. The sound is extraordinary; in high water the spray reaches the viewing platforms. The iron railings around the Cauldron are genuinely necessary — the drop is fatal. Stay behind them.

4
Devil's Cauldron → main entrance (0.3 miles)

The path from the Devil's Cauldron returns quickly to the main entrance via a short steep section. The car park and visitor facilities (toilets, café) are adjacent.

🌧️ Best in Wet Weather

Lydford Gorge is counterintuitively excellent in and after rain — the White Lady Waterfall transforms from a pretty cascade into a thundering spectacle, and the Devil's Cauldron achieves its full roaring, mist-filled drama only when the river is running high. A dry summer visit is pleasant; a wet autumn visit is genuinely extraordinary. Waterproofs are essential in any season — the gorge creates its own microclimate and drips constantly.

Walk Highlights

South end

White Lady Waterfall

A clean 30-metre drop in a single plume — Devon's most dramatic waterfall, named for the white aerated appearance of the water in spate. Most impressive after 24 hours of rain.

North end

Devil's Cauldron

The gorge narrows to a slot, the river is forced through it and compressed into a roaring, mist-filled whirlpool. The most viscerally dramatic natural feature on the walk — genuinely awe-inspiring in high water.

Throughout

Ancient Oak Woodland

The entire gorge is clothed in ancient sessile oak — twisted, mossy, lichened — with ferns covering every boulder and the sound of water as a constant accompaniment. Otherworldly atmosphere.

Historical

Lydford Village

The village above the gorge has a Norman castle (free to enter), a Saxon church and a history as one of Devon's most important medieval towns. Worth 30 minutes before or after the gorge walk.

Dogs at Lydford Gorge

Dogs are permitted in parts of Lydford Gorge but with restrictions. The narrow gorge sections and cliff-edge paths make the full walk unsuitable for dogs — the NT does not permit dogs on the full gorge circular. Dogs are welcome in the car park, the visitor area and on the upper-level paths in some sections. Check with NT staff on arrival for current dog access. If you have a dog, the open moorland and village paths around Lydford itself are freely accessible and the village walk is a good alternative.

Getting There

Lydford is on the western edge of Dartmoor National Park, accessed from the A386 between Okehampton and Tavistock. From the A386, the village of Lydford is signed — the National Trust car park for the gorge is at the bottom of the village, postcode EX20 4BH. There is no bus service to Lydford — car is essential.

Nearby

  • Lydford Castle: A 12th-century Norman tower in the village, free to enter, with a history as one of Devon's most notorious prisons — "Lydford Law" was the mediaeval phrase for being imprisoned first and tried later
  • Brentor: The extraordinary church atop Brentor hill, 5 miles north — one of the most dramatically situated churches in England, visible for miles across the moor
  • Dartmoor walking: Yes Tor and High Willhays — the highest ground in southern England — are accessed from nearby Okehampton, 8 miles north
